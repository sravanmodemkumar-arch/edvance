## 5. Business Rules

- The academic progress report aggregates data from multiple assessment points and computes trend indicators using a weighted moving average that gives more weight to recent exams; for Priya Kumar, the trend analysis considers all four exams (Unit Test 1, Quarterly 1, Half-Yearly, Quarterly 2) but weights the Half-Yearly and Quarterly 2 at 1.5x because they are full-syllabus exams with higher marks reliability compared to the Unit Test which covers only one chapter; the "Predicted Annual Exam Score" range of 87-91% is computed using linear regression on the weighted scores, bounded by a confidence interval that accounts for the standard deviation of past performance; this prediction is explicitly labeled as an estimate and not a guarantee, because Indian parents tend to treat any system-generated number as authoritative, and the platform must avoid creating unrealistic expectations; the prediction model also factors in seasonal patterns observed across the institution's historical data — for instance, if Sri Chaitanya's Class 11 students historically score 3-5% lower in the Annual Exam compared to the Half-Yearly due to increased syllabus coverage, this institutional deflation factor is applied to the projection

- Peer comparison percentile data is one of the most sensitive features on the parent portal and is subject to strict institutional control; each institution admin configures whether percentile data, class rank, and class average are visible to parents — Sri Chaitanya Junior College has enabled full percentile disclosure (common practice in AP and Telangana competitive coaching culture), while some CBSE schools disable rank display entirely following CBSE's guidelines against unhealthy competition; when percentile is enabled, the parent sees their child's position relative to the class distribution but never sees other students' names or individual scores, only aggregate statistics like class average and topper score; the percentile computation uses the standard formula (number of students scoring below the child / total students) multiplied by 100, and ties are handled by average ranking; for Ravi Kumar's CGPA percentile at GCEH, the batch size of 180 CSE students provides a statistically meaningful distribution, and the histogram view helps Mrs. Lakshmi Devi understand that while 7.80 CGPA might sound modest, it places Ravi in the 68th percentile, ahead of more than two-thirds of his batch

- The attendance-versus-performance correlation displayed on Ravi Kumar's progress report is computed using Pearson correlation coefficient across all Semester 5 subjects, and the result is simplified into a parent-friendly label ("Aligned," "Good," "Critical"); for Software Engineering where attendance is 72% and internal marks are 63%, the system flags this as "CRITICAL - both low" because the correlation analysis across GCEH's historical data shows that students with below-75% attendance in a subject score on average 15-20% lower in the external exam for that subject; this insight is particularly actionable for parents of engineering college students because JNTU-H regulations allow attendance condonation only under specific medical circumstances, and the parent can use this data point to have a concrete conversation with their child about prioritizing SE classes for the remaining weeks of the semester; the platform does not imply causation — it frames the relationship as "improving attendance is likely to improve marks" based on institutional patterns — because the actual causes of poor performance may include teaching quality, subject difficulty, or personal issues that attendance alone cannot address

- Coaching progress reports for GATE preparation integrate mock test performance, practice engagement, and topic mastery into a unified view designed for parents who are financing their child's competitive exam preparation; the "Projected Score at GATE 2027" is computed by fitting an exponential growth curve to the mock test scores (68, 72, 74, 78) and extrapolating based on the number of remaining mocks and the typical score trajectory of students who achieved similar scores at this stage in previous GATE cycles on the TopRank platform; the projection is presented as a range (85-90) rather than a point estimate to set realistic expectations; the topic-wise mastery map uses a traffic-light classification — HIGH (above 80% average accuracy across mocks), MEDIUM (60-80%), and LOW (below 60%) — and the "Priority Areas for Improvement" section is auto-generated by ranking topics by their GATE weightage multiplied by the inverse of the mastery level, so Compiler Design (consistently 33% accuracy, carrying 8-10 marks in GATE) is flagged higher priority than OS+CN (70% accuracy but carrying 15 marks); this prioritization logic ensures that the parent's limited intervention bandwidth is directed toward the topics with the highest marks-recovery potential
